Summary of the Book

The Iliad is an epic poem by Homer that recounts the events of the last year of the Trojan War, notably the wrath of the Greek hero Achilles. The story begins with a conflict between Achilles and Agamemnon, the leader of the Greeks, which leads to Achilles withdrawing from the battlefield. As the Greeks lose ground to the Trojans, Patroclus, Achilles' best friend, dons his armor and fights in his place. Patroclus is killed by Hector, the prince of Troy, prompting Achilles to return to battle for revenge.

Excerpt Describing the Shield

"The god began by forming a large and solid shield, adorned on all sides with a triple shining circle; and from its center emanated an immense brilliance. Then, he engraved the Earth, the Sky, and the Sea, the tireless Sun, and the full Moon, and all the constellations that crown the Sky: the Pleiades, the Hyades, and the strength of the giant Orion, and the Bear, also called the Chariot, which turns on the spot and watches Orion, and which alone does not bathe in the Ocean. [...] The god engraved two beautiful human cities on it. In one, weddings were celebrated with feasts, and the young brides emerged from the houses, led through the city by the light of torches. [...] But around the other city, two armies armed for battle had gathered; in this city, war broke out."

Role of the Shield

The shield is described in great detail and is an exceptional work of art symbolizing the entire universe. It depicts scenes of everyday life: weddings, agricultural work, and wars. It is a cosmic representation, a microcosm of human life, capturing both peace and war, creation and destruction. This shield is both a symbol of Achilles' greatness and his tragic heroic destiny. Achilles' shield embodies the richness of human life while highlighting the duality between life and death, and the eternal struggles that shape human existence.
